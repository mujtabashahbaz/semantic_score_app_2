Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging: Chapter 1 Quiz

Test your knowledge on the fundamental concepts of Matter and Radiation as presented in Chapter 1 of *Farr's Physics for Medical Imaging, 3rd edition*. Select True or False for each statement. Feedback and explanations will appear immediately upon selection.

1. The nucleus of an atom contributes the majority of its mass and is composed of protons and neutrons.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True
You were:
The nucleus, composed of nucleons (protons and neutrons), accounts for the majority of the atom's mass.

2. Neutrons have a relative mass of 1 and a +1 charge, while protons have a relative mass of 1 and 0 charge.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False
You were:
Protons have a relative mass of 1 with a +1 charge, while neutrons have a relative mass of 1 with 0 charge.

3. Isotopes of an atom have the same atomic number but a different number of neutrons.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True
You were:
Isotopes are atoms with the same atomic number (number of protons) but a different number of neutrons.

4. The outermost electron shell, known as the valence shell, can hold up to 18 electrons.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False
You were:
The valence shell can only ever have eight electrons within it, forming a complete outer shell octet. The M-shell can hold 18 electrons, but it's not necessarily the valence shell.

5. When an inner shell electron returns to its original shell after being excited, the excess energy is re-emitted as a photon, yielding characteristic X-rays.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True
You were:
This process, where an excited inner shell electron returns to its original shell and re-emits excess energy as a photon, produces characteristic X-rays.

6. All forms of electromagnetic (EM) radiation travel at different velocities in a vacuum.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False
You were:
All forms of electromagnetic (EM) radiation travel at the same velocity as light when in vacuo (2.98 × 10⁸ m/s).

7. Photon energy is inversely proportional to wavelength.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True
You were:
The relationship E = hc/λ (where h is Planck’s constant and c is velocity) shows that photon energy (E) is inversely proportional to wavelength (λ).

8. X-rays are produced when fast-moving electrons interact with a metal target, converting approximately 99% of their kinetic energy into X-rays and 1% into heat.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False
You were:
When fast-moving electrons interact with a metal target, approximately 1% of their kinetic energy is converted to X-rays and around 99% is converted to heat.

9. The process of thermionic emission involves free electrons overcoming the positive attraction of metal anions after a tungsten filament is heated until incandescent.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True
You were:
Heating the tungsten filament in the cathode until incandescent enables thermionic emission, allowing free electrons to escape the metal's attraction.

10. An increased kilovoltage (kV) setting in an X-ray tube increases only the quantity of photons produced, not their effective energy.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False
You were:
Increased kV shifts the X-ray spectrum upwards and to the right, meaning it increases both the maximum and effective energy, as well as the total number of photons.

11. Bremsstrahlung (braking radiation) accounts for 80% of produced X-rays and yields a continuous spectrum of photon energies.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True
You were:
Bremsstrahlung is produced when a bombarding electron is deflected by the nucleus, losing energy as radiation, and it generates a continuous spectrum of X-ray photon energies.

12. Filtration removes higher-energy photons before they reach the patient, primarily contributing to dose reduction.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False
You were:
Filtration involves the removal of *lower-energy* photons, as these mostly contribute to patient dose with little impact on image formation.

13. Attenuation refers to the reduction in the number of photons in a beam after it has passed through material, due to both absorption and scatter.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True
You were:
Attenuation is the overall process where fewer photons are present in a beam after passing through material due to absorption and scatter.

14. The half-value layer (HVL) is the thickness of a given material that will double the intensity of a narrow beam of X-radiation.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False
You were:
The HVL is the thickness of a material that will *halve* the intensity of a narrow beam of X-radiation.

15. The probability of photoelectric absorption decreases with increased incident photon energy.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True
You were:
Photoelectric absorption probability decreases with increased incident photon energy (1/E³) and increases with the atomic number of the material (Z³).

16. In most radiology applications, photon energies are typically greater than 1 MeV, making pair production a significant interaction.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False
You were:
In most radiology applications, photon energies are less than 1 MeV, so only photoelectric and Compton effects are primarily considered. Pair production predominates at much higher energies.

17. Luminescence is the process where a material absorbs energy and re-emits it as visible photons, occurring in relatively cool materials.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True
You were:
Luminescence is the absorption and re-emission of energy as visible photons in cool materials, distinct from incandescence.

18. Fluorescence involves the delayed emission of photons, facilitated by electron traps from impurities in compounds.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False
You were:
Fluorescence involves nearly instantaneous photon emission (10⁻¹⁰ to 10⁻⁷ s). Phosphorescence involves delayed emission, often due to electron traps from impurities.

19. Technetium-99m (99mTc) is ideally suited as an imaging radionuclide because it decays via pure gamma emission (140 keV gamma rays).

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: True
You were:
99mTc's pure gamma emission contributes entirely to image formation, making it ideal for imaging without unnecessary particulate dose.

20. The effective half-life of a radiopharmaceutical in the body is always longer than its physical or biological half-lives.

Show Explanation and Correct Answer

Correct Answer: False
You were:
The effective half-life (teff) will always be shorter than either the physical (tphys) or biological (tbiol) half-lives because both processes occur simultaneously, as defined by 1/teff = 1/tphys + 1/tbiol.
